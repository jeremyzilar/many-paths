# Language Guide: Homelessness and Housing Terms

This guide helps Many Paths use clear, respectful language that fits our audiences: Santa Fe and Albuquerque providers, people with lived experience, funders, and the public. It explains which terms mean what, who prefers what, and when to use each.

---

## Why This Matters

Language shapes how people are seen and treated. Some terms feel stigmatizing or defining; others put the person first. Preferences differ by audience: providers and systems often use one set of terms, advocates another, and many people with lived experience use "homeless" themselves. Our goal is to be accurate, respectful, and understood in each context.

---

## Core Terms and What They Mean

### People and populations

| Term | Meaning | Typical use |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **Person/people experiencing homelessness** | Person-first: the person is not defined by their housing status. | HUD, HMIS, many providers, USICH, formal writing. Widely accepted. |
| **Person/people experiencing street homelessness** | Same idea, narrowed to people living unsheltered (not in shelter). | Many Paths focus: the gap between UniteUS and HMIS. |
| **Homeless person / homeless people** | Direct; "homeless" as modifier. | Common in general public; **many people with lived experience use this**. VA research: 70–73% of middle/low-income adults use "homeless person." |
| **Unhoused** | Without housing; emphasizes lack of shelter rather than "home." | Preferred by some advocates and housed audiences. **Use when quoting or when an org explicitly uses it**; avoid "the unhoused" as a label (AP). |
| **Houseless** | Similar to unhoused; "house" vs "home" to separate structure from belonging. | Some West Coast and advocacy contexts. |
| **The homeless** | Collective noun. | **Avoid.** Treats people as a category, not individuals (AP, Language Please, person-first guidance). |

### Situations and systems

| Term | Meaning | Typical use |
|------|--------|-------------|
| **Street homelessness** | Living unsheltered: outdoors, vehicles, places not meant for habitation. | Santa Fe/ABQ community language; matches where Many Paths operates. |
| **Unsheltered** | HUD term: sleeping in a place not designed for sleeping (cars, tents, outdoors, etc.). | HMIS, PIT counts, federal reporting. |
| **Literal homelessness** | HUD/legal: lacks fixed, regular, adequate nighttime residence; often used for unsheltered + shelter. | Federal definitions, eligibility. |
| **Sheltered** | In emergency shelter, transitional housing, or similar. | HUD/HMIS; contrasts with unsheltered. |
| **Housing insecure** | Broad: risk of losing housing, unaffordable cost, poor conditions, overcrowding. | Broader than literal homelessness; includes at-risk populations. |
| **At risk of homelessness** | Likely to lose housing (e.g., within 14 days) or qualify under other at-risk criteria. | HUD, prevention programs. |

### Other terms to avoid

- **Vagrant, derelict** – Dehumanizing and stigmatizing.
- **Transient** – Implies lack of ties; often used negatively.

## Who Prefers What (Research and Practice)

- **People with lived experience:** In VA research, a majority (70–73%) used "homeless person." Those with lived experience were *more* likely to use "person experiencing homelessness" than those without. Preferences are not uniform; **when possible, follow how the person or community identifies**.
- **Providers, researchers, policymakers:** Most often use "person experiencing homelessness" (39–55%), then "homeless person" (19–41%).
- **HUD / HMIS / federal:** "Individuals/families experiencing homelessness"; "unsheltered" vs "sheltered"; "literal homelessness."
- **Advocates and some media:** Often "unhoused" or "houseless"; person-first phrasing. AP recommends "unhoused" when quoting or when an organization uses it, and advises against "the unhoused" as a collective.
- **Santa Fe / ABQ community:** Your docs and partners use "street homelessness," "experiencing homelessness," and "homelessness" (noun). The Missoula BNL manual (reference in the repo) uses "unhoused" for system status (e.g., "actively unhoused").

## Recommended Usage by Audience

### 1. **Formal and provider-facing (grants, contracts, HMIS-adjacent, City)**

- **Default:** "People experiencing homelessness" / "person experiencing homelessness."
- **When narrowing to your target population:** "People experiencing street homelessness" / "people living unsheltered."
- **Systems/counts:** "Unsheltered homelessness," "sheltered homelessness," "literal homelessness" when matching HUD/federal usage.
- **Avoid:** "The homeless," "the unhoused"; use "unhoused" only when quoting or aligning with a partner who uses it.

### 2. **Public and community (dashboard, website, press)**

- **Default:** "People experiencing homelessness"; "people experiencing street homelessness" when you mean unsheltered.
- **Short form when needed (e.g., headlines):** "Unhoused" or "houseless" is acceptable if space is tight; prefer "people without housing" or "people without homes" where it fits (Language Please).
- **Never:** "The homeless" or "the unhoused" as a label.

### 3. **When reflecting or quoting people with lived experience**

- Use the terms **they** use: "homeless," "unhoused," "houseless," etc.
- If someone says "I'm homeless," keep that in quotes or narrative; don’t "correct" to "experiencing homelessness" in their voice.

### 4. **When aligning with Santa Fe / ABQ partners**

- "Street homelessness" is clear and widely understood locally.
- "Homelessness" (the issue) is standard; "people experiencing homelessness" or "people experiencing street homelessness" (the people) keeps person-first and matches HMIS-adjacent language many providers use.

### 5. **Internal / technical (Airtable, UI labels, data)**

- Be consistent: e.g., "currently unhoused" vs "in shelter" (as in Missoula manual) is fine for status fields if your partners use it.
- For public-facing labels, prefer "Experiencing homelessness" or "Unsheltered" / "In shelter" depending on context.

---

## Many Paths Conventions (Suggested)

- **Mission and narrative:** Prefer "people experiencing street homelessness" and "people experiencing homelessness" so we stay person-first and aligned with HUD/provider language.
- **Topic/issue:** Use "homelessness," "street homelessness," "unsheltered homelessness" as needed for clarity.
- **When a partner or document uses "unhoused":** Use it in that context (e.g., fiscal sponsor materials, BNL references); avoid "the unhoused."
- **Data and UI:** Match your Airtable and dashboard language to what providers and the Coalition use (e.g., "unsheltered," "in shelter," "experiencing homelessness") so it feels familiar.
- **Stories and quotes:** Use the language of the person speaking.

## Quick Reference

| Context | Prefer | Avoid |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Grants, City, HMIS-adjacent | People experiencing homelessness; people experiencing street homelessness | The homeless; the unhoused |
| Public / dashboard | People experiencing homelessness; street homelessness (when relevant) | The homeless; the unhoused |
| Lived experience voice | Whatever the person uses | Overriding their words |
| Headlines / space-limited | Unhoused, houseless, or "people without housing" | The homeless; the unhoused (as label) |
| Data/technical | Unsheltered / sheltered; consistent with partner systems | Inconsistent synonyms in same field |

## Sources and Further Reading

- **AP Stylebook:** Prefer "person experiencing homelessness"; use "unhoused" when quoting or when an org uses it; avoid "the homeless" and "the unhoused."
- **Language Please:** [Homelessness / houselessness](https://languageplease.org/homelessness-houselessness/); [Housing security / insecurity](https://languageplease.org/housing-security-insecurity/). Person-first options: "people without housing," "people without homes," "person experiencing homelessness (or houselessness)."
- **HUD:** "Experiencing homelessness"; "unsheltered" vs "sheltered"; "literal homelessness" for definitions and PIT.
