## Why this work is essential

A shared data system for homelessness coordination is only as strong as the trust providers place in it. That trust starts with a clear, written answer to the question every provider will ask before entering a single client record: who can see this data, and what will you do with it?

Without formal data governance, providers will not participate. They cannot. Their clients have privacy rights. Their organizations have legal obligations. And their staff have been burned before by systems that promised confidentiality and delivered something else.

This contract produces the governance framework, data entry standards, and consent documentation that make it legally and ethically possible for providers to enter real client data into the shared system. It also maps the relationship between this coordination system and HMIS, which is the federally required data system that many providers already use. Without this mapping, providers face the risk of duplicating effort or violating their reporting obligations.

This work is a prerequisite for provider onboarding. No provider should be asked to enter live client data until the City has answered the governance questions in writing.

Federal privacy law (HIPAA) requires formal policies and procedures before collecting protected health information. This contract produces those policies and supports the City in meeting that obligation.

## Background

The coordination system being deployed by the City will hold personally identifiable information (PII) and in many cases protected health information (PHI) about individuals experiencing homelessness in Santa Fe. This includes names, dates of birth, service histories, health conditions, and case notes.

The system uses role-based access controls to separate what different users can see. City staff have access to the full data layer. Provider staff access the system only through restricted interfaces that show them only what is relevant to their organization. No provider can see another organization's client data without explicit coordination through the system.

But technical access controls alone are not enough. Providers need a formal, written agreement with the City about how data is collected, stored, shared, and deleted. Clients need to understand what they are consenting to before their information enters the system. The City needs to know its obligations under state and federal law before it begins collecting sensitive data at scale.

This contract produces all of that.

## What will be delivered

### Provider data mapping session

Before any governance documents are drafted, 59 Projects LLC will facilitate an in-person working session with representatives from the initial participating provider organizations and City staff.

This session is not a presentation. Providers are not told what data the system will collect. Instead, they work it out together. The session is structured to surface three things: what information each type of provider actually needs to coordinate care effectively, what data they are willing and legally able to share with other organizations, and what concerns or conditions would need to be addressed before they would feel comfortable entering real client data into a shared system.

The output of the session directly shapes the data governance framework, data entry standards, and field definitions that follow. Governance built from this conversation will reflect what providers actually agreed to, not what a contractor assumed they would accept. That distinction matters when it comes time to ask providers to sign a participation agreement.

This session is also the right moment to invite the Mayor. The work of building trust among providers, mapping what data will be collected, and working through concerns about client privacy is exactly the kind of community-driven process that deserves civic visibility. The Mayor's presence signals that the City is committed to this program at the highest level, which changes the conversation in the room. Providers who might otherwise hold back are more likely to engage seriously when they see that City leadership is paying attention.

59 Projects LLC will design and facilitate the session, produce a written summary of what was decided, and carry those decisions directly into the governance documents.

### Data governance framework

A formal governance document establishing the rules for data in the coordination system. This document will:

- Define who owns the data. The City of Santa Fe is the data custodian. 59 Projects LLC manages the system under contract. Providers contribute data under the terms of a participation agreement.
- Establish what data can be collected, by whom, and for what purpose. The governance framework enforces data minimization: only information that serves a coordination function is collected. Fields that are "nice to know" but not used are not included.
- Define who can access what. Role-based permissions are documented: what City administrators can see, what provider portal users can see, and what is never visible to anyone outside the City.
- Establish how long data is retained and what happens when a client's record should be closed or deleted.
- Define the process for responding to a data breach or unauthorized access incident.
- Align with City of Santa Fe data policies and applicable state and federal law, including HIPAA.

This document will be reviewed and signed by the City before any provider is onboarded.

### Provider data participation agreement

A short, plain-language agreement for participating provider organizations to sign before accessing the system. This agreement establishes:

- What the provider agrees to collect and enter
- Who at the provider organization has access, and under what conditions
- What the provider is and is not permitted to do with data accessed through the system
- How the provider handles staff departure (revoking access)
- What happens if the provider stops participating

This agreement is designed to be readable and signable by a nonprofit executive director without a lawyer present.

### Data entry standards

Clear, provider-facing standards for what to enter, when, and how. These standards are written for non-technical provider staff, not data administrators. They cover:

- Required fields vs. optional fields. Required fields are the minimum needed for coordination to function. Optional fields provide useful context but are not expected from every provider at every interaction.
- When to enter data. Expectations for timeliness: for example, within 24 hours of an outreach contact, before a case conferencing meeting, and when a client exits the list.
- How to handle common edge cases: clients who use multiple names or aliases, records with missing information, suspected duplicate records, clients who decline to share certain information, clients who request removal from the list.

The standards are grounded in the progressive data capture model: outreach workers are expected to enter less than shelter intake staff, who are expected to enter less than case managers. Standards are calibrated to what each provider type can realistically do, not what would be ideal in a complete record.

### HMIS field mapping

A one-page reference document showing how this coordination system relates to HMIS (the Homeless Management Information System required by HUD for federally funded homeless services). This document:

- Lists which fields in this system correspond to HMIS data elements
- Clarifies which information belongs in this system vs. in HMIS
- Explains how a client record can progress through both systems as the person moves through services: a minimal outreach record in this system can inform a more complete HMIS intake record later
- Makes clear what this system is not: it does not replace HMIS, fulfill HMIS reporting requirements, or eliminate the need for HMIS for providers who are already using it

This document is important for providers who receive HUD funding and are already required to use HMIS. They need to know that participating in this coordination system does not create conflicting obligations.

### Consent documentation

Guidance and template language for providers to use when seeking client consent to share information through the system. This includes:

- A consent form template that can be adapted by individual provider organizations
- Guidance on verbal consent: what to say when a client cannot or will not sign a form, and how to document that a verbal consent was obtained
- Guidance on partial consent: how to handle clients who are willing to be tracked in the system but do not want certain information shared with certain organizations
- Consent language available in English and Spanish

Consent standards are aligned with existing City consent practices, HIPAA requirements, and HMIS consent standards.

### Updated governance for the City's Airtable environment

The previous data governance document (drafted under earlier contracts when a fiscal sponsor was in the contracting chain) will be reviewed and updated to reflect the current structure: 59 Projects LLC as contractor, the City of Santa Fe as data custodian, no fiscal sponsor.

## Assumptions

- The City will coordinate logistics for the provider data mapping session: venue, invitations to provider organizations, and any scheduling support needed to confirm attendance. 59 Projects LLC will design and facilitate the session.
- The City will designate a staff member to review draft documents and coordinate legal review as needed. Legal review by City counsel is the City's responsibility and is not included in this contract.
- The City will provide access to any existing data governance documents, consent forms, or participation agreements from prior contracts for reference.
- The City's Airtable administrator will provide documentation of the technical access controls in place (role permissions, audit logging configuration) to support the governance framework.
- The provider participation agreement template will be adapted by each provider organization before signing; final agreements between the City and individual providers are outside the scope of this contract.
