## The Scale of the Problem

Albuquerque has spent enormous sums on homelessness with outcomes that are getting worse, not better:

- **$35M** spent in FY2021-22 on homeless initiatives
- **$60M** spent in FY2022-23
- **$52.2M** in the current FY2026 general fund budget for the Health, Housing & Homelessness (HHH) department
- **$300M+ total** spent over the last four years
- The FY2026 budget includes funding for **116 separate service contracts**
- Despite all this, Albuquerque's **unsheltered population grew 40%** between 2023 and 2025, outpacing shelter bed growth by 2-to-1
- Total homeless population rose from ~2,394 in 2023 to ~2,960 in 2025

The city also has significant federal funding exposure -- roughly $75.4M in multi-year HUD and other federal grants currently at risk from federal funding changes.

## The Gateway Network (Core Infrastructure)

The city has built a physical network of facilities under the "Gateway" brand:

| Facility | Details |
|---|---|
| **Gateway West** | 660-bed shelter, open 24/7 since 2019, ~5,700 individuals/year. Now operated by Chicanos Por La Causa (CPLC), replacing Heading Home after 6 years |
| **Gibson Health Hub (Gateway Center)** | Medical, behavioral, social services + overnight beds. Open since 2022, ~20,200 individuals/year |
| **Gateway Recovery** | 50-resident pallet home micro-community for recovery, opened May 2025. Operated by Endeavors (San Antonio-based) |
| **Gateway Young Adult** | For ages 18-25, converting former motel. Opening 2026. ~$12M total budget |
| **Medical Sobering Center** | Recently opened at Gateway Center. Operated by Horizons Services |
| **Men's Housing & Treatment Navigation** | 42-bed expansion, operated by Community Bridges Inc. (Mesa, AZ) |

Notable pattern: the city is increasingly bringing in **out-of-state operators** because local providers either can't meet the RFP requirements or have faced accountability issues. This is a source of tension in the provider community.

## The Trust and Accountability Crisis

This is the context behind what Jeremy heard about "low trust within the provider community":

- The **Supportive Housing Coalition of New Mexico** admitted to misusing $200K+ in city housing voucher funds, putting clients at risk of eviction and leaving the city on the hook for back rent to landlords. Contract terminated.
- City Council passed legislation in January 2025 requiring HHH to submit a plan to reduce what was described as a **"confusing process riddled with delays"** for people seeking housing assistance, with mandatory quarterly reporting
- Councilor Renée Grout has been pushing for real outcome tracking on ~$12.4M in annual housing vouchers -- the city currently has weak reporting
- Gateway West had to go out for RFP **twice** before finding an operator -- no local providers responded

A **ProPublica investigation published March 10, 2026** (two days ago) found that despite Keller's anti-criminalization rhetoric, homeless-related arrests have surged dramatically in 2025 -- sidewalk obstruction charges up nearly 6x, unlawful camping charges up from 113 to 704. This is very hot politically right now.

## The Data and Coordination Gap

This is our direct entry point:

- The city's FY2025 budget included just **$100K non-recurring** for development of a technology system to coordinate social services across providers
- There is **no functioning By Name List** and **no real case conferencing** infrastructure
- The New Mexico Coalition to End Homelessness noted that Gateway "hasn't had a huge outsize impact" on actual homeless counts
- The city is running **116 separate service contracts** with no unified data layer connecting them
- Street outreach teams, shelter operators, and housing navigators are all working in separate systems

---

## Our Opportunity

**Two connected threads:**

**1. Santa Fe to Albuquerque knowledge transfer**
Jeremy is building By Name List and case conferencing infrastructure in Santa Fe. Albuquerque is the bigger, messier version of the same problem. There is a real opportunity to share frameworks, lessons, and eventually coordinate across both cities -- but Albuquerque needs trust-building and system stabilization first before they're ready for that.

**2. Unified record management application**
One of the biggest operational pain points for street outreach teams and case managers is adding, managing, and moving records across multiple systems. Albuquerque likely uses **Foothold Technology (AWARDS)** as their HMIS platform. Adding more systems to an already fragmented environment will fail.

The pitch: **a single, unified interface** that:
- Makes it faster and easier for outreach teams to add and update records in the field
- Interfaces with existing systems (HMIS, shelter management, city databases) rather than replacing them
- Progressively enriches records as individuals move through the system
- Feeds real-time data upward so city leadership gets the counts and decision-making data they currently lack

This directly addresses the $100K technology line item in the city budget -- they know they need this, they just haven't found the right solution. The value proposition is **simplification and speed for frontline workers**, with better data as the byproduct.

## Suggested Approach for the Call

- **Listen first.** Ask Charlie what the two overlapping services are that they think aren't working -- the answer will reveal where the real political and operational tension lives
- **Lead with the data background.** She's a research person. Frame everything in terms of what they can measure and what they currently can't
- **Don't over-promise the tech.** Position it as making the existing teams' lives easier, not as a new system requiring a new procurement
- **Use Santa Fe as the proof of concept.** Smaller city, cleaner environment, actively building the infrastructure -- Albuquerque can learn from and eventually connect with it
