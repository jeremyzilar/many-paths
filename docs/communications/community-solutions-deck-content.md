## Slide 3: Core principles

**Two principles guide everything we build.**

### 1. Make the work visible.

Hundreds of organizations in Santa Fe are doing incredible work to help people in need. That work is invisible. It doesn't reach the public. It doesn't shape the narrative about the city.

Many Paths makes that work visible - not just the problem, but the community response.

### 2. Start with real user needs.

Real people inform product decisions from day one. We test assumptions with providers and people experiencing homelessness. We build what's actually useful, not what we think is clever.

[Visual: Two icons or visual representations of each principle]

**Talking point:** "These aren't just values - they're how we differentiate from top-down tech solutions that fail because they weren't built with the people who use them."

### 1. Shared by-name list and case conferencing

Shared by-name list software and case conferencing across providers — fully HIPAA compliant

### 2. Community resource coordination

Making it easy for the public to donate, volunteer, and contribute where it's actually needed

### 3. Make the work visible

Sharing the incredible human-centered work happening across Santa Fe's provider community, grounded in rich, real-time data

## Slide 5: The service model

**Many Paths operates independently. Cities pay for coordination.**

| Many Paths                    | City                            | Providers                        |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Owns technology and data      | Pays for service                | Use the platform                 |
| Runs infrastructure           | Convenes providers              | Participate in case conferencing |
| Provides training and support | Coordinates meetings            | Contribute data                  |
| Produces public storytelling  | Benefits from data and insights | Benefit from coordination        |

**Why this model?**

- Survives political transitions
- Clean data ownership
- Scalable to other cities
- Sustainable revenue model

[Visual: Three-column table or simple diagram showing relationships]

**Talking point:** "This model means that when a mayor changes or a champion leaves, the work continues. That's been a real problem in this field."

## Slide 8: Built to replicate

**Santa Fe is the proof of concept. The model scales.**

Each new city needs a lean team to launch:

- **1 Project Manager** — coordinates providers, facilitates case conferencing, manages relationships
- **1 Communications Lead** — public storytelling, community engagement, donor relations
- **1 Design Researcher** — user research, platform customization, training

The platform, methodology, and playbook are shared across all cities. Local teams adapt to local context.

**What replication looks like:**

- **Year 1:** Santa Fe pilot, full documentation
- **Year 2:** 2-3 additional New Mexico communities
- **Year 3:** Expand to other states with similar needs

[Visual: Timeline or expansion map]

**Talking point:** "This is not a one-off project. It's infrastructure for a movement. Each city needs three people and access to the shared platform. That's how we scale without building a giant organization."

# Demo flow script (2-3 minutes)

## Opening (10 seconds)

"Let me show you what we've built. This is working software - not mockups."

## 1. Public Dashboard (30 seconds)

**Show:** Homepage with DashboardSummary

"This is the public-facing dashboard. Right now, Santa Fe has [X] people experiencing homelessness - [X] in shelter, [X] on the streets, [X] actively seeking placement. This updates in real-time as providers enter data. The public can see this. Policymakers can see this. It's transparent."

## 2. Provider Directory (30 seconds)

**Show:** Providers page

"Here's every provider in Santa Fe with real-time bed availability. [X] beds at [Provider], [X] open right now. Emergency contact numbers. Donate buttons. This is how community members find help - and how they can help."

## 3. By-Name List Concept (30 seconds)

**Show:** People table (or explain if sensitive)

"Behind the scenes - for authorized providers only - is the by-name list. Every person experiencing homelessness, tracked by name. Which providers are working with them. What services they've received. What their goals are. This is how coordination actually happens."

## 4. Community Engagement (30 seconds)

**Show:** Donations page

"And here's how the community engages. Providers post what they actually need - right now. No more guessing, no more unwanted donations piling up. Direct connection between people who want to help and where help is needed."

## Closing (10 seconds)

"That's Many Paths. Coordination for providers. Engagement for the community. Visibility for the work."

# Delivery notes

## On Henri's departure

Let Henri share this himself when it feels natural. When he does, reinforce: "Henri believes so strongly in this approach that he made the decision to join us full-time. That's the kind of commitment we're building on."

## On the ask

Be specific. "$175,000 for a 12-month pilot" is better than "we need funding." You can negotiate later.

## On the demo

Don't apologize for what's not finished. Show what exists with confidence. "Here's what we've built so far" is fine.

## On Built for Zero

Rosanne created this. Frame Many Paths as extending her work: "We're building the technology infrastructure that makes Built for Zero operational in smaller cities."

## On the service model

Emphasize that it survives political transitions - that's a real problem in this field and she knows it.

## On replication

Be concrete: "Each city needs three people: a project manager, a comms lead, and a design researcher. The platform and playbook are shared. That's how we scale without building a giant organization."

## On "Make the work visible"

This is your emotional hook. Hundreds of organizations in Santa Fe doing incredible work that nobody sees. Many Paths changes that. This resonates with providers (who feel unseen) and with Brian (who wants a positive story to tell).

# What this deck does not include

- Detailed technical architecture (not relevant for this audience)
- Long explanations of HIPAA compliance (it's a feature, not the story)
- Extensive problem framing (they know the problem)
- Multiple pages about the "why" of homelessness (preaching to the choir)

Keep it tight. Leave room for conversation. Rosanne is an expert - she doesn't need to be educated, she needs to be convinced this is worth backing.
